# HealthBuddy AI — Final V1 Build Brief

## Decision
HealthBuddy AI V1 should launch as a **small, highly trustworthy public education site** under the HealthBuddy brand.

It should lead with the visitor’s problem, not with the brand story.
The brand should support trust, not dominate the experience.

This is **not** a broad medical knowledge base, not a symptom encyclopedia, not an insurer directory, and not a replacement for clinical care.

The first release should be intentionally narrow, calm, credible, and easy to expand later.

---

## Core Positioning
**HealthBuddy AI helps people understand serious illness, care options, and questions to ask — in plain language, with no agenda.**

The site should feel:
- calm
- professional
- plain-language
- medically conservative
- emotionally respectful
- credible enough for clinicians to share
- useful enough for families to return to

The site should **not** feel like:
- a hospital website
- a government website
- an insurance portal
- a law firm
- a generic startup landing page
- a medical advice service

---

## Primary User Reality
The user is often not browsing casually.
They may be overwhelmed, frightened, sleep-deprived, confused, or facing serious decisions for themselves or someone they love.

The site should meet them there.

The homepage should be problem-first.
Suggested framing:

> **Serious illness is overwhelming. We help you understand what’s happening, what your options are, and what questions to ask — in plain language, with no agenda.**

Then introduce HealthBuddy as the organization/brand behind the resource.

---

## V1 Launch Scope
Build **5 pages** for the first release.

### 1. Home
Purpose:
- orient the visitor quickly
- make the value proposition obvious
- reduce fear and confusion
- route users into the core educational areas
- establish trust fast

Must include:
- problem-first hero
- short explanation of what the site helps with
- quick navigation to core sections
- visible credential/trust layer
- clear educational-only framing
- emergency notice

### 2. What Palliative Care Is
Purpose:
- explain palliative care simply and clearly
- reduce common fear and confusion
- distinguish palliative care from hospice without overcomplicating it
- answer the most common beginner questions

Target:
- concise
- plain language
- myth-clearing
- emotionally steady

### 3. Goals of Care
Purpose:
- serve as the main decision-support education page
- explain major care-goal concepts in careful, non-directive language
- help families prepare for conversations with clinicians

Should include sections on:
- what “full code” means
- what comfort-focused care means
- tradeoffs and questions to ask
- planning documents as subsections:
  - POLST
  - advance directives
  - DNR / DNI
  - healthcare proxy / surrogate decision-maker

Important:
- planning documents must include clear **state-specific caution**
- do not imply one universal form/process applies everywhere

### 4. Comfort Measures
Purpose:
- provide conservative plain-language education about common supportive care approaches
- help families understand practical comfort-focused concepts without drifting into diagnosis or individualized treatment advice

Content approach:
- one consolidated page
- no deep symptom library
- no “common causes” sections that drift toward diagnostic interpretation
- use careful language such as:
  - “some people find…”
  - “commonly used supportive approaches…”
  - “ask your care team about…”
- avoid prescriptive phrasing like “you should…”

Possible content areas:
- pain comfort basics
- nausea comfort basics
- constipation comfort basics
- breathlessness comfort basics
- skin / pressure care basics
- caregiver support / simple caregiving realities if it fits cleanly

### 5. About / Transparency
Purpose:
- establish human trust
- explain who created the site
- explain why it exists
- explain what it is and is not
- make the trust model explicit

Must include:
- authorship / leadership identity
- relevant credentials displayed clearly
- mission in plain language
- what the site does not do
- no-diagnosis / no-personalized-advice boundary
- whether data is collected or not
- whether ads or sponsorship exist or not
- content review / update philosophy
- if AI-generated videos or visuals are used, explain methodology at a high level

---

## Explicitly Excluded From V1
Do **not** include these in the first release:
- condition-specific pages
- symptom deep-dive pages
- insurer / insurance-company contact directory
- large external resource directory
- broad disease library
- anything that feels half-finished or thin

Reason:
A smaller site with strong pages builds more trust than a wide site with shallow pages.

---

## Videos in V1
Videos are important strategically, but for V1 they do **not** need a standalone page unless there is already enough finished material to justify it.

Recommended V1 approach:
- place videos within relevant pages where useful
- feature them lightly on Home if available
- create a dedicated video library later when the content volume justifies it

If AI-generated videos are used:
- disclose that clearly and calmly
- do not create false realism or false implication of actual patient footage
- make sure presentation remains educational, respectful, and non-sensational

---

## Trust and Safety Requirements
These are non-negotiable.

### 1. Educational-only disclaimer
Every page should clearly state a version of:

> This information is for general education only. It is not medical advice and does not replace your relationship with your healthcare team.

### 2. Emergency notice
Every page should include a visible emergency notice, worded carefully, such as:

> This site is for education, not emergency care. If you think you may be having a medical emergency, call 911 or seek urgent medical help.

### 3. Conservative clinical language
Use:
- plain language
- non-directive wording
- low-drama tone
- no overpromising
- no diagnostic framing
- no individualized treatment recommendations

### 4. State-specific planning-document caution
For POLST, advance directives, DNR/DNI, and proxy documents:
- clearly note that forms and rules vary by state
- encourage checking local/state requirements and healthcare teams

### 5. No false institutional signaling
Do not use:
- seals
- badges
- fake institutional motifs
- hospital mimicry
- government-adjacent styling intended to imply official status

### 6. Real trust signals instead
Prefer:
- clear authorship
- real credentials
- transparent boundaries
- clear sources
- careful writing
- visible review/update practices

---

## Design Direction
The site should look simple, elegant, calm, and credible.

Recommended feel:
- warm but restrained
- medical-adjacent without pretending to be institutional
- highly readable
- strong spacing and typography
- mobile-friendly
- accessible
- static and fast

Avoid:
- clutter
- dense navigation
- loud startup aesthetics
- overly corporate SaaS patterns
- fake “official” healthcare branding

---

## Technical Constraints
V1 should be easy to build and ship quickly.

Preferred implementation:
- static HTML/CSS or equally lightweight approach
- no backend required for V1
- simple deployment
- easy to edit and expand later

Technical priorities:
- fast load speed
- mobile responsiveness
- accessible typography
- clean information architecture
- maintainability without heavy tooling

---

## Content Standard
Every sentence should pass this test:

> Would a careful clinician be comfortable if a patient brought this page into an appointment?

Also:
- sources should be specific when cited
- no vague “studies show” language
- no fluffy wellness copy
- no exaggerated certainty
- no manipulative emotional framing

---

## What Success Looks Like
A family member lands on the site late at night, scared and uncertain.
Within minutes, they feel:
- less lost
- more oriented
- more prepared for a real conversation with the care team
- more trusting of the resource
- not misled into thinking they received medical advice

Clinicians should feel:
- this is careful
- this is shareable
- this is not embarrassing
- this is appropriately bounded

---

## Final Strategic Summary
HealthBuddy AI V1 should be a **problem-first, trust-first, small public education site**.

Launch the smallest version that feels:
- finished
- credible
- clinically careful
- emotionally intelligent
- expandable

Do not try to win by covering everything.
Win by being clear, honest, and useful.
